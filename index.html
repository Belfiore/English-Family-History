FitzMaurice & Andrews

The Story Behind This

After Grandma's service, there was a large collection of photos out on the table. I took pictures of a handful that caught my eye.

Recently started running them through AI research tools. Studio names on the backs of cards, handwriting on the reverses, newspaper clippings — each one gave up a little more information. The more I dug, the further back it went. What started as a few old photos turned into a family line that traces back through Victorian London, through a Scottish noble family, and back to a King of Scotland in the 1400s.

This is what we've pieced together so far.

Note: there may be incorrect or missing information. This covers Grandma's lineage only — Grandad's side is not included.

Every quarter of that shield represents a different branch of the family including our direct line — British Prime Ministers, the first Field Marshal in British Army history, a Victorian actress whose portrait hangs in the Met in New York.

The Family Crest

The coat of arms carried by the FitzMaurice Earls of Orkney is a quartered shield — each section representing a different noble bloodline united through marriage and inheritance over centuries. The red diagonal cross on ermine is the FitzMaurice family's own mark, our direct bloodline. The ship on blue represents the ancient Earldom of Orkney, tracing back to the Norse jarls. The Hamilton lions connect the line to the Dukes of Hamilton and, through them, to Princess Mary Stewart, daughter of King James II of Scotland. Above the shield, the oak tree with a saw through its trunk is the Hamilton crest — the blade inscribed with the word "Through", a motto earned when a Hamilton ancestor rescued a Scottish king from ambush. The motto reads "Through Courage."

Clues from the Collection

Camden Town, c. 1865

The earliest photograph in the collection was taken at J. Perriman's studio near the Mother Red Cap pub in Camden Town. A mother and young daughter — likely an earlier FitzMaurice generation — establish the family's North London roots. The reverse of the card reads: 'Camden Town School of Photography, Commission No. 925.'

Edith Madeleine FitzMaurice, c. 1899

A circular medallion portrait inscribed on the reverse 'Edith Madeleine Fitzmaurice c.1899.' Edith was Reg's sister — Patricia's aunt. The photograph was found among the family collection and is one of the earliest identified portraits.

Edward Dixon FitzMaurice

Edward Dixon FitzMaurice, a Church of England clergyman, was photographed at the prestigious Stuart Studio, 47 & 49 Brompton Road, Knightsbridge. His clerical collar, wire-framed spectacles, and Knightsbridge address speak to comfortable Victorian London life. He is Reg's father and Patricia's grandfather.

Lang Sims Woman, Brixton Road

An unidentified young woman photographed at Lang Sims studio, 437 Brixton Road SW and 9 Church Street, Folkestone. Possibly Edith or another FitzMaurice family member. The studio was active in the late Victorian and Edwardian period.

Reg FitzMaurice — Patricia's Father

Reg FitzMaurice, Patricia's father. This formal portrait shows him as a young man, c. 1910–1920, wearing a three-piece suit with a bowtie. Reg gave Patricia away at her wedding in 1950. He was living in Hayes, Middlesex at the time. His father was the clergyman Edward Dixon FitzMaurice.

Reg, Bobs and their Father

The reverses of two photographs confirm key family identities. One reads 'Reg, Bobs and their Father Edward Dixon Fitzmaurice.' The other reads 'Auntie Bobs, South Africa.' These handwritten inscriptions are the primary evidence linking Reg back to Edward Dixon.

Babs in South Africa

'Auntie Babs' emigrated to South Africa, photographed on a verandah with wrought iron railings and subtropical plants typical of Cape Colony architecture. She returned to England for Patricia's wedding in 1950. The photo reverses confirm her identity.

The Gaiety Theatre Connection

Connie Gilchrist, wife of the 7th Earl of Orkney, was a star of the Gaiety Theatre — Victorian London's most celebrated venue. Her marriage into the FitzMaurice peerage was a society sensation of its era. She was painted by Whistler, and her portrait hangs in the Metropolitan Museum of Art.

The Korean War Earl

Two newspaper clippings kept in the family collection. The first from the Evening News: 'Earl from Korea Left a Fortune — He Scrubbed Decks.' The second: 'Earl of Orkney Marries' — Cecil married Rose Silley at St Mary's, Brixham, Devon in November 1952. These clippings prove the family knew about their connection to Cecil.

The Photo Collection Spread

The wider collection of photographs and cabinet cards laid out together. Additional postcards, unidentified portraits, and snapshots from across the family's history — many still waiting to be identified.

Key Discoveries

1430–1460

King James II of Scotland

The family line traces back to King James II of Scotland, who reigned from 1437 until his death in 1460. His daughter Princess Mary Stewart married James Hamilton, 1st Lord Hamilton. From the Hamiltons came the Dukes of Hamilton, from the Dukes came the Earls of Orkney, and from the Earls came the FitzMaurice line that leads directly to Patricia. The evidence: Burke's Peerage records the Hamilton–Stewart marriage; the FitzMaurice succession to the Earldom is recorded in the London Gazette; and the family's own wedding album confirms Patricia as a FitzMaurice.

Prime Minister 1782–1783

The Prime Minister Who Ended the American Revolution

William Petty-FitzMaurice, 1st Marquess of Lansdowne, served as Prime Minister from 1782 to 1783 and negotiated the Treaty of Paris that ended the American Revolutionary War. He shares the FitzMaurice name through the same Anglo-Norman dynasty — 'son of Maurice,' tracing back to Maurice FitzGerald's invasion of Ireland in 1169. The Lansdowne FitzMaurices became Marquesses; the Orkney FitzMaurices became Earls. Both branches descend from the same medieval Irish-Norman stock.

1666–1737

George Hamilton, 1st Earl of Orkney — Britain's First Field Marshal

George Hamilton (1666–1737) was created 1st Earl of Orkney in 1696 by William III. He fought at the Battle of the Boyne and served as a general under Marlborough at Blenheim, Ramillies, Oudenarde, and Malplaquet. In 1736 he became the first person in British history to hold the rank of Field Marshal. He married Elizabeth Villiers, William III's mistress. The Earldom he created passed through the Hamilton and FitzMaurice families for three centuries.

1865–1946

Connie Gilchrist — The Gaiety Girl Who Became a Countess

Constance Gilchrist (1865–1946) was one of the most famous faces in Victorian London — a child star at the Gaiety Theatre, painted by Whistler ('The Gold Girl'), photographed by every major studio, and the subject of intense public fascination. In 1892 she married the 7th Earl of Orkney, Edmond FitzMaurice. The Duke of Beaufort gave her away. She went from actress to Countess in a single afternoon. Her portrait 'Harmony in Yellow and Gold' hangs in the Met in New York.

1919–1998

Cecil O'Bryen FitzMaurice — The Last Earl

Cecil 'Ginger' FitzMaurice (1919–1998) was an R.A.S.C. truck driver who served in North Africa, Italy, France, Germany, and Korea. He was scrubbing mess-decks as punishment on the troopship HMT Devonshire when he learned he had inherited the Earldom of Orkney and a fortune of £42,893. He was Amanda's 3rd cousin once removed — connected through the 5th Earl. The newspaper clippings were kept in the family collection because Patricia's family knew Cecil was kin. He died without issue in 1998, ending the FitzMaurice chapter of the Earldom forever.
